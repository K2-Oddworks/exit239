EXIT 239 — DISPATCH NO. 02

The Quiet Part

Eleven acres of nothing. Forty miles of silence. It was the best six months of his life — right up until it wasn't nothing anymore.

THE LAND, GETTING LESS EMPTY

Warren bought eleven acres off a dirt spur past the Zzyzx turnoff for the same reason everybody eventually gives, if you ask them honestly, which is that he had stopped being able to stand the sound of other people's lives leaking into his. The neighbor's dog in the old place. The upstairs footsteps. The bass from a car two blocks over, decoding itself through his walls at 1 a.m. like a language he'd never asked to learn.

Eleven acres of nothing. A double-wide he paid to have hauled in on a flatbed. A well. A generator. Forty miles of silence in every direction, if you didn't count the Preserve, which nobody did, because who counts land nobody's allowed to build on.

The first six months were the best of his adult life. He is not embarrassed to admit this, to himself, out loud, to the empty room, which is a thing he'd started doing and which felt less like loneliness and more like finally getting to hear his own opinions.

He slept outside more nights than not, a cot dragged onto the hardpan, because the stars out here didn't behave like stars anywhere else he'd lived — no glow on the horizon, no competing light, just the whole unfiltered inventory of the sky laid out like it had been waiting for someone to finally stop and look. Middle of nowhere, he'd say to no one, testing the phrase the way you test a plank before you put weight on it. Middle of nowhere. It held.

Once a week he drove the forty minutes into town for groceries, and he came to enjoy that too, in a way he hadn't expected — not the town itself, which was nothing, a gas station and a market and a diner that closed at three, but the reminder. People existed. They pushed carts and complained about the heat and ignored him entirely, and there was something almost tender in being ignored by strangers after a week of nobody at all. He didn't mind the drive out. He didn't even mind the drive back, forty minutes with a cooler wedged in the passenger seat so the milk wouldn't turn and the ice cream wouldn't be soup by the time he pulled in — a small tax, an inconvenience he'd decided to love instead of resent, because it was proof of the bargain he'd made and gotten the better end of. That was the charm of it. The whole charm of the middle of nowhere was that it cost you something to keep it that way.

The generator hum used to be the only mechanical sound for miles. In month seven, Warren starts hearing a second one, faint, somewhere north, and tells himself it's an aircraft, or the Preserve's water pump, or nothing.

By month eight it's not one generator. It's several, overlapping, a chord instead of a note.

By month nine there are headlights at night that aren't his, sweeping across the scrub half a mile out, twice, three times a week, and Warren stands at his window in his boxers at 11 p.m. like a man watching a slow leak find the bottom of the boat.

The envelope arrives with the mail he drives forty minutes to collect. It is not addressed to him by name so much as by parcel number, which is its own kind of insult. Letterhead: DESERT VISTA ESTATES AT ZZYZX — HOMEOWNERS ASSOCIATION. A logo of a stylized sun that looks like it was designed in an afternoon by someone's nephew.

Inside: a welcome packet. A map — an actual map, laminated, with lot lines drawn across land Warren has personally walked, bled on, buried a dead coyote in — showing two hundred and eleven residential parcels. A newsletter, Volume 1, Issue 1, with a headline about an upcoming "Founders' Mixer" at the not-yet-built clubhouse. A notice, separate, in bolder type, informing Warren that his mailbox does not conform to Section 4.2 of the Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions, and that non-compliant structures are subject to a $50 monthly assessment until remedied.

He reads Section 4.2 twice. It specifies an approved mailbox vendor. There is an approved mailbox vendor. Warren sits at his kitchen table for a long time with the newsletter open to a stock photo of a family riding bicycles somewhere that is not here, will never be here, and thinks about the particular kind of man who drafts a Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions document for fifty thousand people who all moved to the same forty square miles of nowhere specifically to get away from documents like this one.

The knock comes on a Tuesday. Warren doesn't get knocks. He opens the door already annoyed and finds no one — just the fading percussion of small feet on hardpan, two kids sprinting for a golf cart idling forty yards off, a woman at the wheel not quite hiding her laugh, lifting a hand at him that could be an apology or could just be a wave, hard to tell from this distance, and then they're gone in a spray of gravel dust, off toward whatever this now is.

There are children here. That's the part that sits with him longest, longer than the mailbox, longer than the HOA letterhead. Not just people. Generations of people. Cul-de-sacs. Birthday parties. A future.

By month fourteen there's a name for it that isn't Desert Vista Estates, not in the way people actually say it. The papers call it Solace, California, population estimates ranging from thirty to sixty thousand depending on which agency is guessing, the fastest-growing unincorporated community in the state, a phenomenon, a piece on the evening news wedged between a wildfire update and a weather forecast that is, out here, always the same.

The van has a satellite dish folded up on its roof like a held breath. It parks where Warren's turnoff meets the county road, which used to be the only road, which used to be nobody's road but his.

The reporter is younger than he expected, professionally warm, holding the microphone the way people hold things they've been taught to hold rather than things they've learned to hold. Behind her, a cameraman frames Warren's double-wide, his eleven acres, the water tower going up two miles out that someone is apparently already calling historic.

"We're talking to some of the original homesteaders out here at Solace," she says, mostly to the camera, then pivots the mic toward him, warm, practiced, a little rehearsed even in its spontaneity. "I have to ask — you must be thrilled about the Supercenter. They broke ground last week, did you hear? A real grocery store, gas, pharmacy, all of it, twelve minutes from your door. No more driving all the way out to town."

She says it like she's handing him something. Like she's been saving it.

Warren looks past her, at the water tower, at a golf cart cresting a low rise a half mile off, at the whole gleaming laminated future of it, and thinks, absurdly, of a cooler in a passenger seat, ice going soft around a carton of milk, forty minutes each way, the closest he'd come in a decade to missing anyone.

"What made you decide to settle all the way out here, at the very start, before any of this?" she asks, when he doesn't answer the first question.

"I moved out here to be left alone," he says.

The reporter smiles, waits half a beat for the rest of it, the part where he explains, the part where it turns into a story about grit or peace or finding himself. It doesn't come. She glances at the cameraman. The wind picks up a little, carrying, faintly, from somewhere north, the overlapping hum of several generators running at once.
